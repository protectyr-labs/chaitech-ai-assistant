How to use this workbook

This is a print-friendly customer discovery interview workbook built around Rob Kenedi's framework. The 8-section flow, the 4 key questions, and the 15% talk rule are universal. The product-specific examples are mine; you replace them with yours.

What you need to customize

Anywhere you see one of these placeholders, fill in your own:

  • [YOUR PRODUCT] — what you actually sell or are building.
  • [YOUR ROLE] — your title and elevator pitch (e.g., "founder of [company]").
  • [YOUR INDUSTRY / DOMAIN] — the world your buyer lives in.
  • [YOUR ICP] — your ideal customer profile.
  • [YOUR PRIMARY PAIN] — the specific pain your product solves.
  • [YOUR COMPETITORS / COMPLEMENTS] — tools or services your prospects already use.

Read tab by tab. Print the verbatim script (tab 2) and the quick reference cards (tab 6). Keep the branching tab (tab 3) open on a second screen during real calls.

The 3 discipline rules (memorize)

  1. Talk less than 15% of the call. If you are speaking, you are not learning.
  2. Position yourself as a researcher, not a seller. Drop "we offer" or "we provide" from your vocabulary during this call.
  3. Look for gaping neck wounds (urgent, must-fix-now pain), not vitamins (nice-to-have).

The 3 levels of pain (Rob's vocabulary)

Vitamin

Nice to have. Prospects say they want it. They will not buy. Conversation is polite but goes nowhere.

Painkiller

Real pain. Prospects will pay to make it stop. Budget exists, even if hidden.

Gaping neck wound

Urgent, must-fix-now. Prospects will pay a lot, immediately. This is your target.

Q4 (the magic wand question) is your magnitude probe. Quantify in dollars or hours.

The verbatim script

Every line you say is written out. Fill in placeholders with your specifics. Treat everything else as the default phrasing.

Section 1 — Opening (0 to 2 min)

YOU TALK 90%. The only section where you do most of the talking.
"Hi [first name], thanks for taking the time today. Before we get into it, two quick housekeeping things."
Pause one second. Smile.
"First, would you be okay if I record our conversation? I find that I take much better notes when I am not typing during the call. Nothing leaves my own notes."
Wait for explicit verbal answer. Do not start recording until they say yes.
If they say YES to recording (most common)
"Great, thank you. I am starting it now."
Click record. Confirm visible "recording" indicator.
"Second thing, I want to be transparent up front. This is not a sales call. I am running a research program where I am talking to 40 [YOUR ICP — e.g., founders / CFOs / IT leaders / etc.] to understand how [YOUR PRIMARY PAIN AREA] actually shows up in their business. I am not going to pitch you anything today. If something I do turns out to be useful to you afterwards, we can have that as a separate conversation. Sound good?"
Wait for "yes" or "okay." Move to Section 2.
If they say NO to recording
"No problem at all, I get it. I will take notes by hand. Same promise on confidentiality, nothing leaves my notes."
Open a notebook. Resign yourself to slower note-taking.
"Quick second thing, just to set expectations. This is not a sales call. I am running a research program where I am talking to 40 [YOUR ICP] about [YOUR PRIMARY PAIN AREA]. I am not going to pitch you anything. Sound good?"
If they ask "what is this for? are you trying to sell me?"
"Fair question. I am [YOUR ROLE] running [YOUR COMPANY]. We do [YOUR ONE-SENTENCE WHAT-WE-DO]. I am at a stage where I am trying to understand the market better, so I am running 40 research interviews. Today, I am literally just listening. If at the end you want to hear about what I do, I will tell you. If not, no follow-up. Either way, I learn something."

Section 2 — Context (2 to 5 min)

YOU TALK 25%. Light fact-finding.
"So tell me a bit about your role. How long have you been at [Company], and what does your week typically look like? I am especially curious about [YOUR DOMAIN] because that is where my work lives."
Listen. Note their tenure, formal remit, who else touches your problem area.

Section 3 — Pain Discovery (5 to 17 min) — Rob's 4 key questions

YOU TALK 10%. The longest section. Listen hard. Follow tributaries.

Q1 — The concrete story

"Walk me through the last time you faced [YOUR PRIMARY PAIN] — something where you stopped and thought, this is not routine."

Why this question: asks for a specific memory. Specific memories produce truthful data. Opinions produce socially-acceptable answers.

If they go blank ("nothing comes to mind")
"Fair enough, that is actually useful data. Let me ask it differently: what is the most annoying thing in [YOUR DOMAIN] that has landed on your team in the last six months? Even something small."

If still blank after the second prompt, they likely do not feel acute pain. Move to Q3 to map the org and Q4 to gauge if there is latent magnitude.

Q2 — What they tried

"What have you done to solve it? What happened?"

Why: uncovers current solutions, workarounds, and the unmet gap.

Listen for: existing solutions and tools

If they mention a vendor, an internal team, or a DIY workaround, probe:

"How is that working out? What is it covering well, and what is it not covering?"

Their answer maps the gap your product might fill — or confirms it would not.

Q3 — Who else cares

"Who else is affected by this problem?"

Why: maps the stakeholder ecosystem. Surfaces other Champions, Influencers, and the Decision Maker.

Q4 — The magic wand (the magnitude question)

"If this problem disappeared tomorrow, what would change for you?"

Why: separates vitamins from painkillers from gaping neck wounds. Force the quantification.

If they give a quantified answer (gaping neck wound signal)

Examples of strong answers (across industries):

  • "I would not lose 5 hours a week to this."
  • "My team could finally do strategic work."
  • "The board would stop asking about it."
  • "We would not lose deals because of it."
"That is helpful. Can you put a rough dollar figure on what that would be worth to you in a year?"
If they say "it would be nice" (vitamin)
"Okay. What would the breathing room let you do that you cannot do today?"

If still vague, mark them as vitamin-level and move on. Not every prospect is a fit.

Section 4 — Current state and magnitude (17 to 22 min)

YOU TALK 20%.
"Today, what is your team actually spending on this? In hours, in tools, in external help — even rough numbers help me."
Wait. Be comfortable with silence.
"And who in the company actually does this work? You, an internal hire, an external vendor?"
"What would make this 10 percent better for you? And what would make it 10 percent worse?"
When they give you a number, repeat it back: "So you are spending about [X] a year on this. Did I get that right?"

Section 5 — Solution framing (22 to 25 min)

YOU TALK 60%. Brief. Optional. Never a pitch.
Only enter Section 5 if it feels natural. Skip it if they are watching the clock.
"Let me describe roughly what we do, and you can tell me where it fits or does not. Sound okay?"
If they say yes:
"[YOUR 3-SENTENCE PRODUCT DESCRIPTION. Frame as a category and a differentiator, not a feature list. Example structure: We do [category] for [ICP]. Our differentiator is [one specific thing]. We are also [building / planning] [adjacent thing]. That is it.] Does anything you just told me connect to that, or not really?"
If they push back ("we have [competitor] already")
"Great, those are real tools. What I do [sits on top of / is different from / complements] them — I help [specific gap]. Does that gap exist for you, or is your team handling it well?"
If they ask "how much does it cost?"
"Honest answer: I am not going to quote you on this call. I want to think through your specific situation and come back with something that fits. For today, I am just trying to learn. Is that okay?"
If they ask "can you show me a demo?"
"Yes, absolutely — but not on this call. Today is for me to listen. If after I send you a one-pager you want a demo, I will set that up as its own conversation."

Section 6 — Stakeholder mapping (25 to 28 min)

YOU TALK 25%. Surface the 8 personas. Probe Saboteur explicitly.
"If you wanted to bring something like what I described into [Company], who else would be part of that decision?"
"Who would be most excited about it?" (your Champion candidate)
"Who would push back the hardest, even if it was a good idea?" (your Saboteur surface)
"Who actually approves the spend?"
"Last question on this: is there anyone whose work might actually shift if you brought in something like this — someone who has to live with it day to day?"

Section 7 — Close and referral (28 to 30 min)

YOU TALK 80%. Two asks. Stop.
"This was really useful, thank you. Two quick things before I let you go."
Pause one second.
"First, who else in your network do you think I should talk to as part of this research? I am trying to get to 40 conversations and quality intros are worth way more than cold outreach. Anyone come to mind?"
"Second, would it be useful if I sent you a short one-pager on what we do, just so you have it if it ever becomes relevant?"
"Thank you again. Really appreciate the time."

Section 8 — After the call (next 10 min)

SOLO. Do this immediately.
  1. Stop the recording. Confirm it captured.
  2. Open your CRM (or spreadsheet) and update the row for this prospect.
  3. Set: pain level (vitamin / painkiller / gaping_neck_wound), validated_pain (yes/no), validated_product (yes/no), willingness_to_pay (none / maybe / yes), saboteur_risk (low / medium / high).
  4. Notes: one quote, the real pain (your inference), the stated pain (what they said).
  5. Send the thank-you email within 2 hours.
  6. If they offered an intro, follow up within 24 hours with a one-line "ready when you are" message.

Branching — what to say when the conversation goes off-script

Real calls do not follow the script. Below are the most common deviations and a scripted reaction for each.

Pre-call deviations

They no-show

Wait 5 minutes. Then send this email:

"Hi [name], hope everything is okay. I had us down for [time] and did not see you on the call. Happy to reschedule if a different day works better — here is my booking link: [calendly]. If the timing is no longer right, no worries."

If no reply in 3 days, mark them dropped. Do not chase a third time.

They reschedule on short notice
"No problem, life happens. Send me a couple of times that work and I will book one in."

During the opening

They say "I have a hard stop in 15 min, can we do this quickly?"
"Absolutely, let me compress. I will skip the context section and jump straight into the substance. Two questions for you, and we will be out in 15."

Then ask Q1 and Q4 only. Skip Q2, Q3, Section 4, Section 5, Section 6. Hit the close.

They say "no" to recording AND seem suspicious
"Totally fine, I understand. Honestly, the easiest version is if I just take notes. And to be 100 percent clear — there is no sales motion here. I am literally trying to learn. If at the end you have feedback for me on whether this was useful, I would value that."

During pain discovery

They give a long monologue you cannot follow

Let them finish. Then:

"That is a lot of useful context, thank you. Let me make sure I am tracking the most important thread. You said [echo back the most concrete thing they mentioned]. Can you walk me through that specific situation in more detail?"
They ask you a question instead of answering
"Happy to come back to that. Let me first finish the question I had — and then we can dig into yours. So you were saying [last thing they said]..."
They mention a competitor or existing tool
"Great, that is a real tool. What do you wish [tool name] did better? Or what part of [your domain] is not covered by [tool name] for you?"

Never bash a competitor. Make their existing tool the entry point to a deeper conversation.

They say "we are too small for this"
"That is helpful. What size company do you think this kind of work starts to make sense for? I am genuinely asking — that is part of what I am trying to figure out."
They say "we don't have budget"
"Fair, and again — I am not pitching today. I am asking how you think about that decision. Is it that there is no budget category for this, or that the existing budget is allocated, or that the leadership has not seen the case for spending?"

During solution framing

They get excited and want to buy NOW
"That is a wonderful response and I want to honor it properly. Today was a research call so I do not have a real proposal in front of you. Let me follow up tomorrow with a proper conversation about what an engagement might look like, and we can move at your pace from there."
They say "send me a proposal" without much enthusiasm
"Happy to. Just so I scope it right, can I ask one more thing first — what would have to be true in the proposal for it to be a yes for you?"
They flat-out say "this is not a fit for us"
"Got it, thank you for being direct. Can I ask: what would be a fit, from your perspective?"

Then close warmly and ask the referral question. Their network may still include people who do fit.

During the close

They volunteer to introduce you to multiple people
"That is incredibly generous. The most useful thing for me would be one or two introductions where you think the fit is strongest. I would rather have two great conversations than five lukewarm ones. Who would be the top one or two on your list?"

After the call

They ghost after the call

Do nothing for 7 days. Then one short follow-up:

"Hi [name], no expectation of a reply on this. Just wanted to circle back in case the timing for a referral or proposal feels different now. If not, no follow-up — just thanks again."

Pre-call briefings

Before every interview, spend 5 to 10 minutes building a one-page briefing on the prospect. The difference between a generic call and a call where you sound like you did your homework is exactly this 10 minutes.

What a good briefing contains

Quick orientation (read 5 min before the call)
  • Their role at the company.
  • Likely champion archetype: Finance / Engineer / Management / Mixed.
  • Recommended Q1 phrasing tailored to their industry.
  • Saboteur risk: who in their org might lose if you win? Often IT or an existing vendor.
Company snapshot
  • Domain, industry, sub-industry, country, HQ location.
  • Employee count and revenue estimate.
  • Tech stack hints (BuiltWith, Wappalyzer, job postings).
  • Tools they likely use that are in your category.
Recent signals
  • Funding rounds, mergers, leadership changes.
  • Recent news mentions (Google news 2026 + their company name).
  • Recent job postings. Strong signal that budget is actively flowing into the function you sell into.
  • Anything regulatory or industry-specific they recently dealt with.
The contact themselves
  • Name, title, LinkedIn URL.
  • Open the LinkedIn URL and skim the last 3 posts. Note any topic to mention organically. Do not force it; only use if you can do it naturally.
  • Mutual connections (LinkedIn shows this when you visit the profile).
  • Tone they project (formal / casual / thought-leader / lurker).
Conversation hooks (use sparingly)
  • "I noticed [Company] is hiring a [role]. What is the job you are trying to fill?" (only if hiring)
  • "I saw your post about [topic]. How is that working out?" (only if recent and you read it)
  • "I see you have been at [Company] for [years]. What has changed for you on the [your domain] side in that time?" (always works)

Tooling options

Pick whatever fits your workflow:

  • Manual: a Notion page or Google Doc per prospect, populated 5 to 10 min before the call.
  • Spreadsheet: one row per prospect with columns matching the briefing sections above. Quick to scan.
  • CRM auto-pull: if your CRM (Attio, HubSpot, NocoDB, Notion) has enrichment, pull what it has and fill manual gaps. Many of us are already storing intel — this is just surfacing it for the call.
  • Scripted generator: if your CRM has an API, write a 100-line script that pulls a record by ID and renders a markdown briefing. (I built one for my CRM; happy to share the pattern.)

Build your top 50 contacts list

This is Chris Carder's exercise from his Welcome Day Masterclass: who are your top 50 network connections? Approached for discovery interviews, this becomes your starter pool.

Three pools to pull from

Pool A — Cold but enriched (your CRM, lead lists, LinkedIn search)

15 to 20 names from cold sources where you have basic enrichment data. These need verification before outreach (visit their website, find their LinkedIn).

Sources to consider:

  • Existing leads in your CRM that have not been worked.
  • LinkedIn Sales Navigator search filtered to your ICP.
  • Industry directories or association member lists.
  • Recent job-posting signals (LinkedIn jobs filter by your relevant role).

Outreach template: cold (research framing, not sales).

Pool B — Warm network from your existing context

10 to 15 names from people who already know you or have a mutual connection.

Sources to consider:

  • ChaiTech Cohort 7 founders (they are themselves SMB decision-makers).
  • Past clients or colleagues who would take a 25-min call.
  • People you met at conferences, meetups, or alumni events in the last year.
  • Your LinkedIn 1st-degree connections who match your ICP.

Outreach template: warm (mention the mutual context).

Pool C — LinkedIn 1st-degree filtered to your ICP

15 to 20 names from your existing LinkedIn network filtered carefully.

How to filter (30 minutes on LinkedIn):

  1. Open Connections.
  2. Filter by location (your target geography).
  3. Filter by current company size (your ICP — typically 51 to 500 for SMB plays).
  4. Filter by title (the buyer titles for your category).
  5. Pick the most plausible to take a 25-min research call from you.

Outreach template: warm or in-person ask if you see them in person.

Quick reference cards

Print this tab on a single page. Cut into cards. Tape up where you can see during a call.

Card 1 — Rob's 4 questions

  1. Walk me through the last time you faced [your primary pain].
  2. What have you done to solve it? What happened?
  3. Who else is affected by this problem?
  4. If this problem disappeared tomorrow, what would change?

Card 2 — The 3 discipline rules

  1. Talk less than 15% of the time.
  2. Position yourself as a researcher, not a seller.
  3. Look for gaping neck wounds, not vitamins.

Card 3 — The 7 do-nots (Rob)

  • No talking about your product (in pain discovery).
  • No yes/no questions.
  • No confirmation-biased questions.
  • No ignoring tributaries.
  • No avoiding the magnitude question (Q4).
  • No confounding users, stakeholders, customers.
  • No closing during discovery.

Card 4 — Vitamins / painkillers / wounds

  • Vitamin: nice to have. They will not buy.
  • Painkiller: real pain. They will pay.
  • Gaping neck wound: urgent. They will pay a lot, immediately.

Card 5 — The 8 personas (Rob)

  • Champion — wants what you have
  • End User — uses it daily
  • Decision Maker — signs the contract
  • Financial Approver — approves spend
  • Influencer — formal or informal
  • Saboteur — has something to lose
  • Evaluator — assesses fit
  • Procurement — runs buying

Card 6 — Map YOUR product

Fill in for your own offering:

  • Sits on top of: [X]
  • Alongside: [Y]
  • Replaces: [Z]
  • Not a competitor to: [W]

Card 7 — After every call (10 min)

  1. Stop recording.
  2. Update your CRM row (stage, pain_level, validations).
  3. Notes: 1 quote + real pain + stated pain.
  4. Thank-you email within 2 hours.
  5. Intro follow-up within 24 hours (if offered).

Card 8 — Pre-call (5 min)

  1. Open prospect's LinkedIn, skim last 3 posts.
  2. Open their website, verify name + title.
  3. Google their company name + "news 2026".
  4. Check if they are hiring (signals budget).
  5. Confirm legal/permission basis for your outreach.
